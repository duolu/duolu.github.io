Establishing A Hand-Gesture Research Platform

for Behavior Biometrics and Cognitive Robotics (HGRP)

I am Duo Lu, a graduate student under the direction of Professor Dijiang Huang in the School of Computing, Informatics, and Decision Systems Engineering at Arizona State University. I am conducting a research study to evaluate the security and usability of using in-air-handwriting for user identification, authentication, and writing content recognition purpose. This study is supported by NSF CCRI:CNS 1925709. I am recruiting individuals to write strings in-the-air for my research which will take approximately 10 minutes to one hour. The participants include adults with ability to write.

I am inviting your participation, which will involve writing strings in-the air. You have the right not to answer any question, and to stop participation at any time. You must be 18 or older to participate in the study. The following is a detailed plan of the data collection protocol. In this study, two devices are going to be used to capture your hand motion (shown in Figure 1): a wearable device (a custom-made data glove with inertial sensors) and a contactless 3D camera (the Leap Motion controller). The datasets to be collected contain the following parts. You will be designated to participate in one or more of the following procedures.

Figure 1: Devices to be used: a data glove (left) and a 3D camera (right)

(1) Sign-up and Sign-in: We will ask you (as a normal user) to create two distinct meaningful strings and write them in-the-air, one as an account ID and the other as an account passcode. Such a string may include alphanumeric letter, character in a language other than English, or meaningful symbols such as five pointed stars. To keep your privacy, please create new ID and passcode instead of writing ID or password you have used before, and please do not use the ID or password created for this study in the future as an ID or password. For each string, we will ask you to write it with 5 repetitions for registration and another 5 repetitions for multiple login attempts, which simulates the normal sign-up and sign-in procedure with the two strings used as account ID and password (shown in Figure 2). As explained in the other datasets, your in-air-handwriting will be videotaped and will be imitated by others to evaluate the capability of in-air-handwriting for authentication and identification purpose. We will provide face masks and other means to keep your identity confidential in the video upon your request. This procedure will take about 5 to 10 minutes. You may have a few minutes to get familiar with writing in-the-air and practice a little bit.

(2) Spoofing with Semantic Leakage: We will ask you (as an imposter) to write the same string of the ID or the passcode generated by the users other than you. You will be informed with the semantic meaning of the strings, but you will not see the original users writing the ID and password in the air in person or through a video. This procedure simulates spoofing attack with semantic leakage. We will ask you to write each string with 5 repetitions. This procedure will take about 5 minutes for a pair of strings for an ID and a passcode. You can choose to write more than one pairs of strings. You can choose to write more if you would like to contribute more.

(3) Spoofing with both Semantic and Visual Leakage: We will ask you (as an imposter) to imitate the in-air-handwriting of the ID and passcode generated by the users other than you. You can watch a video recording of the in-air-handwriting of the original users and you will be informed with the semantic meaning of the in-air-handwriting. We will ask you to write each string with 5 repetitions. This procedure will take about 5 to 10 minutes for a pair of strings for an ID and passcode, depending on the complexity of the passcode. You can choose to write more than one pairs of strings. You can choose to write more if you would like to contribute more.

(4) Long-Term Persistence Study: We will keep collecting the sign-in in-air-handwriting from you (as a normal user) for a period of around 4 weeks. We will ask you to create two strings for an account ID and passcode, and write each of them 5 repetitions for registration, similar as that in the first procedure. After that we will ask you to write the two strings 5 repetitions in a single day as a session, and at around 2 sessions each week, in total 10 sessions. This procedure will take about 5 minutes for one session.

(5) In-Air-Handwriting Recognition Study: We will ask you (as a designated writer) to write around 20 designated words and 20 designated sentences in the air with 5 repetitions each. The words and sentences are from articles published in newspapers, magazines, and online. You will be required to write in the eligible format. This procedure will take about 15 minutes in total. You can choose to write more if you would like to contribute more.

(6) In-Air-Handwriting Stroke Study: We will ask you (as a designated writer) to write every combination of two symbols in our basic symbol table (see appendix A, there are in total 112 * 112 combinations). You will be asked to write a few combinations, not all of them. This procedure will take about 10 minutes for 20 combinations. You can choose to write more if you would like to contribute more.

(7) In-Air-Handwriting User Behavior Study: We will ask you (as a designated writer) to write a few string composed with a sequence of basic symbols in the air with 5 repetitions. This procedure will take about 5 minute.


(8) Usability Survey: We will ask you (as a normal user) to fill a survey on the usability of in-air-handwriting based sign-in system with the two devices. The survey questions are in Appendix B. This procedure will take about 5 minutes.

Figure 2: An illustration of sign-up and sign-in procedure with wearable device and 3D camera

Your participation in this study is voluntary.  If you choose not to participate or to withdraw from the study at any time, there will be no penalty. You will receive compensation of $25 per hour of participation. A minimum $15 (i.e., one hour) will be given if the total length of the data collection for one participant is less than one hour. You can choose the dataset you want to participate. The minimum requirement is participating only in dataset 1, 4, 7 and 8, which takes around 40 minutes in total. You can choose to contribute more for dataset 2, 3, 5 and 6 if you would like to contribute more. The data generated by you and your response in the survey will significantly benefit the academic research community on in-air-handwriting related research since currently there are very limited datasets and studies available. There are no foreseeable risks or discomforts to your participation. As mentioned before, we advise you neither to write existing ID or password you use in the past nor to use the ID and passcode written for this study in the future, so as to eliminate the risk of leakage of your personal identity and passwords.

We would like to video record your hand motion in-the-air, which may from multiple angles and may have your other part of body in the background. It will not be recorded without your permission. Please let me know if you do not want it to be recorded; you also can change your mind after the data collections starts, just let me know. The video record of your hand motion, the raw data generated by you, and your response to the survey questions will be kept confidential at ASU. However, processed hand motion trajectories of the fingertips and finger joints will be released as open datasets in anonymous ways. The results of this study may be used in reports, presentations, or publications but your name will not be used, and the results will only be shared in aggregate form.

If you have any questions concerning the research study, please contact the research team at: Dijiang Huang <dijiang.huang@asu.edu> or Duo Lu <duolu@asu.edu>. If you have any questions about your rights as a subject/participant in this research, or if you feel you have been placed at risk, you can contact the Chair of the Human Subjects Institutional Review Board, through the ASU Office of Research Integrity and Assurance, at (480) 965-6788. Please let me know if you wish to be part of the study.

The symbols with footnotes have more than one way of writing. Different ways of writing is generally considered as different symbols. Hence, there are repetitions of characters in this table but they actually mean different symbols.

See a full list of the stroke decomposition of each symbol in the following document.

https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1hJ4oLyzFu-LzermGBxyfTdn2GNBVffq0J8oaga7tkPg/edit?usp=sharing

[1] "A" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the first vertical stroke is written downward or upward.

[2] "B" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the vertical stroke is written first or last.

[3] Uppercase "C" and lowercase "c" are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting.

[4] "D" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the vertical stroke is written first or last.

[5] "E" may have four different ways of writing, depending on the sequence of vertical stroke and the three horizontal strokes. See the stroke decomposition document.

[6] "F" may have three different ways of writing, similar to "E".

[7] "H" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the horizontal stroke is the last stroke or not.

[8] "K" may have three different ways of writing. Also, uppercase "K" and lowercase "k" are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting.

[9] "M" may have three different ways of writing. See the stroke decomposition document.

[10] "N" may have three different ways of writing, similar to "M".

[11] "O" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the circle is clockwise or counter-clockwise. Also, uppercase "O" and lowercase "o" are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting. Note that "0" (digit zero) is also indistinguishable from "O".

[12] "P" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the vertical stroke is written first or last, which is similar to "D".

[13] "Q" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the circle is clockwise or counter-clockwise, which is similar to "O".

[14] Uppercase "S" and lowercase "s" are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting.

[15] "X" may have two different ways of writing, depending on which stroke is the first. Also, uppercase "X" and lowercase "x" are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting.

[16] "i" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the dot is the first stroke or the last stroke.

[17] "j" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the dot is the first stroke or the last stroke, which is similar to "i".

[18] "q" may have two different ways of writing. See the stroke decomposition document.

[19] "t" may have three different ways of writing. See the stroke decomposition document.

[20] "y" may have two different ways of writing. See the stroke decomposition document.

[21] "1" may have three different ways of writing. The first two ways are shown in the stroke decomposition document, while the third way is essentially the same as "↓".

[22] "@" may have two different ways of writing, depending on whether the outer circle is clockwise or counter-clockwise, which is similar to "O".

[23] "%" may have four different ways of writing, depending on whether the circle is clockwise or counter-clockwise and whether the slash is written first or last. See the stroke decomposition document.

[24] "`" (grave accent) and "." (dot) are indistinguishable for in-air-handwriting.

[25] "*" may have four different ways of writing, depending on the stroke sequence. See the stroke decomposition document.

[26] "↙" is also "/" (slash).

[27] "↘" is also "\" (backslash).

[28] The last row is about arc stroke. All of them are half circle arcs. The difference is the direction.

Appendix B: Survey Questions

Consider that you are required to login through gesture interface without a keyboard or touch -screen to type a password. You are required to login to a virtual site or unlock your device with either a wearable device or a camera that can track your hand movements.

 Section 1: User Experience. Please choose the most suitable option for each question.

1. The content of the in-air-handwriting passcode is easy to memorize.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

2. The content of the in-air-handwriting passcode is difficult to guess for attackers.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

3. The in-air-handwriting passcode is difficult to be known / seen by attackers at login.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

4. It is difficult for attackers to mimic your writing if the passcode content is leaked.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

5. The in-air-handwriting login method is easy to learn and register.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

6. The in-air-handwriting login method is fast and efficient under gesture input interface.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

7. If the passcode is leaked, it is easy to change / revoke the passcode.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.

8. I will choose the in-air-handwriting as my primary login method over gesture interface.

(A) Strongly Agree.    (B) Agree.    (C) Neutral.    (D) Disagree.    (E) Strongly Disagree.
